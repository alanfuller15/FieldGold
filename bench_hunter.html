The tool flags flat ground in the bench band. Whether a flag is a real gold bench is a call you make on-site. Here's what separates a true old channel from ordinary flat ground:

Rounded gravel, not angular

The single best tell. Water-worn, rounded stones sitting well above the current water mean a stream once ran there. Angular, sharp rock is slope wash off the valley wall — it means little. If the flat is rounded creek gravel, you're likely on an old channel.

A distinct step, then a riser below

A true terrace has a flat tread and a steeper drop below it down to the modern channel. That's the tool's "strong" flag. A flat that just grades gently into the slope is weaker — could be floodplain, could be wash.

Reddish, oxidized, or cemented old gravel

Ancient gravels often weather to a rusty color or cement together. Old, stained, packed gravel high on the bank is a promising sign it's been sitting a long time — long enough to concentrate gold.

Where the pay sits on a bench

As on the active stream, gold rode down to the old bedrock and sits at the gravel–bedrock contact. On a bench, that contact may be exposed in the cut-bank face — probe or dig to find how deep bedrock is, and work that layer.
