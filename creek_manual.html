One

The one idea everything hangs on

Gold is roughly 19 times heavier than water and about 7 times heavier than the average rock around it. Every rule that follows is a consequence of that single fact.

Gold doesn't travel like the gravel it's mixed with. It sinks, it lags behind, it drops out the instant the water loses the energy to carry it, and it works its way down through everything looser than itself until it hits something it can't pass. If you internalize "gold is trying to stop moving and get to the bottom," you can derive most of the rules yourself instead of memorizing them.

That's the difference between someone digging random holes and someone reading water. Every time you look at a stretch of creek, the question in your head is: where did the water slow down, and where did the heavy stuff get trapped?

Two

How gold moves — flood vs. trickle

Gold enters a creek from an eroding source and is then on a one-way budget: it only moves when water is energetic enough to push it, and each move is shorter than the same-sized pebble would travel, because it's so much denser. Over time this sorts gold downward and concentrates it in predictable traps.

The flood does the work

The few days a year of high, brown, roaring water do more geological work than all the calm months combined. This is the most important thing separating real creek readers from everyone else: you read the creek at low water, but the gold sits where the flood dropped it. The productive line is often not under today's gentle trickle — it's under the big cobble bar that only has water screaming over it during breakup.

Two energy regimes matter. Everyday flow moves light gravel and positions fine gold day to day. Flood flow is what actually moves gold and rearranges the creek. To read a creek you have to picture it at ten times its current volume and ask where that force loses its grip.

Three

The anatomy of a bend

You can't read what you can't name. A bend is the single most legible feature on a creek, because it splits the flow into a fast side and a slow side — and gold drops on the slow side.

Key terms, all visible above. The thalweg is the fast thread of deepest flow; it wanders bank to bank as the creek bends. The cut bank is the eroding outside of the bend — fast and deep, and it doubles as a free cross-section you can read. The point bar is the gravel built on the slow inside — your best-odds, easiest-dig target. And the pay streak is the concentrated ribbon of gold, usually sitting on or just above bedrock, running roughly parallel to flow.

Four

The trap catalogue

Every trap below is really the same idea wearing a different hat: the water slowed, or the gold hit a barrier. Learn them as a family and you'll stop needing the list.

The full family: inside bends (slack water on the point bar); behind boulders and ledges (slack pockets just downstream, plus the upstream face of a ledge); bedrock crevices and potholes — especially cracks perpendicular to flow, the single richest trap; the upstream head of any bar or tailout, because gold drops at the first opportunity; gradient breaks, where a steep reach flattens and the water abruptly loses carrying capacity; and confluences, where a tributary meets slower water and heavies pile up.

Benches — the fossil pay streak

Creeks migrate and cut downward over time. A bench is an old creek gravel deposit now stranded above the current water level — sometimes well up the hillside. It was once the creek bottom, and it can hold gold that's been sitting there since that old channel was active. Rounded gravel sitting high above the water is the tell; angular gravel is just local slope wash and means little. Benches are the most-overlooked target because most people walk right past them to work the active stream — and for Fishhook, the documented record says the benches held the richest ground (Section 7).

Five

Reading depth to bedrock

The whole point of reading is to avoid muscling cobble you didn't have to. Since the pay streak sits on bedrock, "how deep is bedrock here?" is the question that decides where you dig. Read it, cheapest-effort first, before you commit.

Read exposed bedrock first. Walk the stretch and mark every place bedrock shows — in the bed, as ledges, as outcrops. Bedrock runs in a consistent direction and tilt over a short reach, so two or three exposures let you project where it dips just under the gravel — your minimum-overburden dig.

Read the creek's shape. Bedrock is shallow at inside bends, upstream bar heads, just below ledges and boulders, and where the channel narrows. It's deep on outside bends, wide flats, and the middle of big open bars.

Read the cut bank. The outside-bend cut bank is a free vertical slice through the whole deposit — you can often see overburden, then a change in the gravel, then the bedrock line, and read total depth before digging.

Probe, then trust the material change. A steel rod gives a hard, repeatable stop at bedrock (a buried boulder stops in one spot but not 18 inches away). As you near bedrock the material packs tighter, gets wetter and clayier, and the black sand climbs — that's your "you're in the pay zone" signal.

True vs false bedrock

If the layer that stops your shovel crumbles into diggable chunks, or your rod punches through it into more gravel, it's false bedrock — a packed clay or hardpan that traps some gold but hides more pay beneath it. True bedrock is continuous solid rock that matches the outcrops nearby. Always test before you call a hole finished.

Six

Sampling to find the pay streak

Amateurs dig one hole where it looks good and judge the whole creek by it. Experts sample systematically to find the edges of the pay streak, then follow it. The streak is a ribbon; your job is to map it.

The routine: dig or pan a line of holes running bank to bank, sampling each all the way to bedrock (a shallow sample tells you almost nothing). Count the colors in each pan — more colors mean you're near the streak's center, fewer mean you're on its edge. Then test upstream and downstream from your best hole to confirm the streak's direction, and stay on it. You're not mining yet; you're building a little contour map of where the values rise and fall. That map is the pay streak.

This is your app's home turf

Following a pay streak is cross-sectional sampling to resolve a signal — exactly the spatial-reasoning your Field Brain evaluation model is built around. Treat the physical creek like a dataset you're sampling to find an edge.

Seven

Fishhook Creek — the verified story

Everything below is corroborated by USGS, Alaska DGGS, the Alaska Earthquake Center, or NOAA/NWS — not inferred. Three forces built the ground you'll work: a gold source, a seismic engine, and a flood engine, all over a glacial foundation.

Verified · the gold source

The Hatcher Pass pluton — a zoned ~74-million-year-old quartz-diorite-to-tonalite body — underlies the headwaters of Willow Creek and Fishhook Creek, and most of the gold deposits occur in these rocks. Early prospectors worked placer gold specifically around Fishhook Creek by hand. Your placer gold has a proven lode parent right at the top of your own drainage.

USGS / DGGS Talkeetna Mountains geology; Willow Creek district records.

Verified · the seismic engine

Your creek sits on the Castle Mountain fault, which runs along the base of Hatcher Pass and is considered one of the most important active faults in all of Southcentral Alaska — the only fault in southern Alaska with both historic seismicity and Holocene surface faulting (it generated the 1984 M5.6 Sutton quake). The region also rode through the 1964 M9.2 megathrust, after which downcutting and bank erosion increased in adjusted areas.

Alaska Earthquake Center; USGS OFR 98-480 / OFR 01-504; DGGS PIR 2017-2.

Why the fault matters to your pan: active uplift and faulting keep forcing the creek to cut downward. Downcutting is exactly what strands old gold-bearing gravels as benches above the modern channel — the fossil pay streaks from Section 4. The tectonics are actively manufacturing your best-odds, most-overlooked target.

Verified · the flood engine

Your drainage floods hard and episodically. The Little Susitna at the Hatcher Pass Rd bridge — which Fishhook feeds — has a documented peak crest of 12.45 ft on 21 Sept 2012 and 10.97 ft in Aug 2006, against a moderate-flood stage of 10.9 ft. Regionally, Alaska's biggest non-outburst floods come from snowmelt, rain-on-snow, and atmospheric rivers — with an atmospheric river present on 83% of extreme-flood days.

NOAA/NWS gage LSOA2; USGS SIR 2025-5056.

Why the floods matter to your read: when Section 2 tells you to "raise the water and read the flood," you're now picturing a real, documented ~12-foot crest — the actual events that positioned the gold in Fishhook's gravels. The trickle you'll stand over in summer never moved a flake.

Verified · the glacial foundation

The valley was shaped by ice: in the Willow Creek district, Quaternary glacial deposits and alluvium form the valley floors, and across the Mat-Su the extent of the Naptowne glaciation largely controls where sand and gravel deposits sit. On a comparable district creek, the record notes the richest ground was on the benches, not the active channel.

USGS Bulletin 933-C / 592-H; Mat-Su Borough Mineral Resources Assessment.

Put together: gold enters high from a proven pluton, active faulting and glacial base-level change strand it in benches as the creek downcuts, and episodic floods place it in the traps. Hunt the benches first, read the creek as flood-built, and remember the source is upstream and local — so coarser gold rides nearer the headwaters.

Eight

The walk-up routine

When you reach a stretch, run this before you touch a shovel. This sequence is creek reading.

  1. Walk the whole stretch first. Don't dig the first tempting spot. Build a picture.
  2. Map the bedrock. Note every outcrop and ledge; establish which way bedrock strikes and dips.
  3. Read the bends. Mark point bars (deposition, easy digging, good odds) and cut banks (free cross-sections).
  4. Scan for benches. Look up the banks for flat steps of rounded gravel above the water — Fishhook's downcutting makes these likely, and the record says they held the richest ground.
  5. Find the slack points. Behind boulders and ledges, at gradient breaks, at confluences, at bar heads and tailouts.
  6. Picture the flood. Raise the water toward that documented ~12-ft crest. Where does the current thread go then? What's underwater and taking the force?
  7. Rank spots by (odds of gold) × (ease of reaching bedrock). A bar head with bedrock 18 inches down beats a cut-bank base under four feet of cobble — especially with kids along.
  8. Probe your top 2–3, confirm bedrock depth, then sample the winner to bedrock, clean the crevices, and pan. Let the color count send you to the next hole.
The whole thing, one more time

Gold is trying to stop and sink. Find where the flood slowed and where the heavy stuff got trapped on or in bedrock, and you've found the gold. Read the flood, not the trickle. Follow the values, don't judge on one hole. Bedrock crevices are the vault; point-bar heads and benches are the easy money.
